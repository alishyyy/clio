The first Unix system was developed in 1969 by Ken Thompson at Bell Labs, written in assembly language for a DEC PDP-7.
The C programming language was created by Dennis Ritchie between 1969 and 1973 at Bell Labs to rewrite Unix.
The term "bug" in computing originated in 1947 when Grace Hopper found a moth causing problems in the Harvard Mark II computer.
Linus Torvalds created Linux in 1991 as a free alternative to MINIX while he was a student at the University of Helsinki.
The first email was sent by Ray Tomlinson in 1971. He chose the @ symbol for email addresses.
The vi text editor was created by Bill Joy in 1976 while he was a graduate student at UC Berkeley.
BSD Unix began as a series of patches to AT&T Unix at UC Berkeley in 1977.
Richard Stallman launched the GNU Project in 1983 to create a free Unix-like operating system.
The first version of the TCP/IP protocol was deployed on ARPANET in 1983.
The World Wide Web was invented by Tim Berners-Lee in 1989 while working at CERN.
The first release of Python was made by Guido van Rossum in 1991.
The first commit to Git was made by Linus Torvalds on April 7, 2005.
The Apache web server was created in 1995, named because it was "a patchy" server made from patches.
The first version of Perl was released by Larry Wall in 1987 as a Unix scripting language.
The first release of Firefox (then called Phoenix) was made in 2002.
The term "open source" was coined in 1998 by Christine Peterson during a strategy session in Palo Alto.
The first version of MySQL was released in 1995 by Michael Widenius and David Axmark.
The SSH (Secure Shell) protocol was invented by Tatu Ylönen in 1995 after a password-sniffing attack at his university.
The LAMP stack (Linux, Apache, MySQL, PHP) became popular in the late 1990s as a free web development platform.
Ken Thompson and Rob Pike created the UTF-8 encoding over dinner in a New Jersey diner in 1992.
Margaret Hamilton coined the term "software engineering" while working on the Apollo program at NASA.
The first computer mouse was invented by Douglas Engelbart in the 1960s and was made of wood.
The first hard drive (IBM 350) was introduced in 1956 with a capacity of 3.75 MB.
Ada Lovelace wrote what is considered to be the first computer program in 1842 for the Analytical Engine.
The Ruby programming language was created by Yukihiro Matsumoto in 1995 to make programmers happy.
The first version of JavaScript was created by Brendan Eich in just 10 days in 1995.
The first webcam was created at the University of Cambridge to monitor a coffee pot in 1991.
The first computer virus, called Creeper, was created in 1971 by Bob Thomas at BBN Technologies.
The famous "404 Not Found" error comes from room 404 at CERN, which housed the web's first servers.
The ZIP file format was created by Phil Katz in 1989 and released with PKZIP compression utility.
The first version of Windows (1.0) was released in 1985, two years after its announcement.
The first dot-com domain name ever registered was symbolics.com on March 15, 1985.
The first commit message in Linux was "Initial revision of Linux" by Linus Torvalds.
The PNG image format was created in 1995 as a free alternative to GIF.
The term "hacker" originally meant someone who made furniture with an axe.
The first computer game, Spacewar!, was created in 1962 at MIT on a PDP-1.
VIM (Vi IMproved) was released by Bram Moolenaar in 1991 as an enhanced version of vi.
The first version of PHP was created by Rasmus Lerdorf in 1994 to track visits to his online resume.
The famous "Konami Code" was created by Kazuhisa Hashimoto in 1986 for Gradius.
The ENIAC computer was completed in 1945 and had 17,468 vacuum tubes, weighing 27 tons.
The first transistor was demonstrated at Bell Labs in 1947 by Bardeen, Brattain, and Shockley.
The first commercial modem (Bell 101) was released in 1958 with a speed of 110 bits per second.
The BASIC programming language was created by John Kemeny and Thomas Kurtz in 1964 at Dartmouth.
The first RAID system was developed by David Patterson, Randy Katz, and Garth Gibson at UC Berkeley in 1987.
The AWK programming language was created in 1977, named after its creators Aho, Weinberger, and Kernighan.
The famous "You have mail" notification was introduced in BSD Unix in 1978.
The first version of Emacs was written by Richard Stallman in 1976 at MIT's AI Lab.
The famous "sudo" command was created by Bob Coggeshall and Cliff Spencer in 1980 at SUNY/Buffalo.
The first release of Debian Linux was announced by Ian Murdock on August 16, 1993.
The Bourne Shell (sh) was developed by Stephen Bourne at Bell Labs in 1977.
The famous "fortune" program was written by Ken Arnold and was first included in BSD Unix in 1979.
The X Window System was developed at MIT in 1984, leading to modern GUI interfaces on Unix systems.
The infamous "Year 2000 Problem" was first noticed by Bob Bemer in 1958.
The "grep" command was created by Ken Thompson, named after the ed command g/re/p (globally search a regular expression and print).
The first version of sed (stream editor) was developed by Lee E. McMahon at Bell Labs in 1973.
The first version of make was written by Stuart Feldman at Bell Labs in 1976.
The cron daemon was written by Ken Thompson in the 1970s, derived from the Greek word for time, "chronos".
The first commercial implementation of TCP/IP was released in 1983 on a VAX system.
The BIND DNS server was created by four UC Berkeley students in the early 1980s.
The first release of Red Hat Linux was made by Marc Ewing in 1994.
The famous "pipe" concept in Unix was proposed by Doug McIlroy at Bell Labs in 1964.
The first version of SCSI was standardized in 1986, originally called SASI.
The IRC (Internet Relay Chat) protocol was created by Jarkko Oikarinen in August 1988.
The famous "chmod" command was part of the original Unix implementation in 1969.
The core utilities (coreutils) package was created as part of the GNU Project in 1992.
The "ls" command's color output was introduced in the early 1990s to help distinguish file types.
The /etc directory name is thought to mean "et cetera" although some argue it means "editable text configuration".
The first version of the GNOME desktop environment was released in 1999 by Miguel de Icaza and Federico Mena.
The KDE desktop environment was started in 1996 by Matthias Ettrich.
The first version of the RPM Package Manager was released by Red Hat in 1997.
The first Linux swap partition was implemented by Linus Torvalds in 1991.
The proc filesystem was first implemented in Unix 8th Edition in 1985.
The famous "man" pages were created by Dennis Ritchie and Ken Thompson in 1971.
The syslog protocol was developed by Eric Allman as part of the Sendmail project in the 1980s.
The first version of Xfce was released by Olivier Fourdan in 1996.
The first implementation of NFS (Network File System) was released by Sun Microsystems in 1984.
The first Linux distribution, MCC Interim Linux, was released in February 1992.
The infamous "rm -rf /" command protection was added to GNU rm in the early 1990s.
The Intel 4004, the world’s first microprocessor, was released in 1971, paving the way for modern computing.
VisiCalc, introduced in 1979, became the first spreadsheet program and is often credited with sparking the personal computer revolution.
The IBM PC, launched in 1981, set the standard architecture for personal computers throughout the 1980s and beyond.
The first digital camera was invented in 1975 by Steven Sasson at Kodak, marking the beginning of digital photography.
In 1997, IBM’s Deep Blue defeated world chess champion Garry Kasparov in a historic match, highlighting the potential of computer intelligence.
The Mosaic web browser, released in 1993, was the first graphical browser for the World Wide Web, making the internet accessible to the masses.
VMware, founded in 1998, pioneered virtualization technology, which laid the groundwork for modern cloud computing.
The IBM Simon, released in 1994, is recognized as the first smartphone, combining mobile phone capabilities with PDA functions.
The Altair 8800, launched in 1975, is widely regarded as the first commercially successful personal computer.
Smalltalk, developed in the 1970s, was one of the earliest object-oriented programming languages, influencing many modern languages.
The first Supercomputer, the CDC 6600 built in 1964 by Seymour Cray, was the fastest computer of its time and set new standards for performance.
Apple’s introduction of the iPhone in 2007 revolutionized mobile computing, creating a new era for smartphones.
